Problem Definition

One of the advantages of defining the problem with others is the opportunity to re-frame the problem. In the 1978 Art of Problem Solving, Russell Ackoff illustrates the example of the “slow elevator problem.” Hotel guests complain to the manager that the elevator is too slow. The Manager consults an engineer who defines the problem mechanically and proposes the obvious solution of replacing the elevator engine at great expense. But the manager digs deeper and hires a psychologist who reframes the problem as “the wait is annoying.” Then it suddenly becomes obvious that hanging a mirror outside of the elevator for people to gaze in will reduce frustration more cheaply. By framing the problem differently, suddenly you discover a more actionable opportunity to solve it.

If you're interested in learning more about Problem Definition, here are a few suggestions for further reading and watching:

  • HBR Article, Are You Defining the Right Problem?
  • Alph Bingham, Problem Definition Video
  • Defining a Problem, Crash Course Kids
  • Positive Deviance Field Guide
One cannot come up with workable solutions until one has defined, as concretely as possible, the problem to be solved together with those affected and with experts.

Human-centered Design

Family 100 was a project to help bring about a deeper understanding of the experiences of families living in urban poverty in Auckland, New Zealand. The project engaged with families that were selected to represent the diversity of the population living in poverty. The study participants were regular users of the Auckland City Mission’s foodbank service. Participants provided detailed information on a wide range of relevant topics including: housing, food, services, education, justice, debt, employment, health and support networks. The project developed this tool that offered a human centred perspective and fostered empathy, challenged assumptions and identified opportunities for change.

Data Analytical Thinking

In an effort to reduce New Orleans’s murder rate, Mayor Mitch Landrieu created a unit in city government dubbed the innovation team or i-Team to tackle the problem. Using data on murder rate, crime rate, educational attainment, unemployment rate, and recidivism rate by neighborhood and dating back to 1960, the team uncovered a significant correlation between unemployment and violent crime (and thus recidivism). The data showed that a majority of murders in New Orleans were committed by a small and identifiable set of people in a few neighborhoods as the result of petty disputes. Municipal agencies instituted various programs to train and hire ex-offenders in an effort to reduce the likelihood of the formerly incarcerated reoffending. The Team’s work led to a decline in the murder rate by 20% between 2012 and 2013, and the number continues to drop. In 2018, murders in New Orleans were at their lowest level in almost fifty years.

Evidence Review

Knowing what works is an important way to identify possible solutions. The skill of evidence review is the process of assessing possible solutions using academic research, grey literature and practical experience to inform policy-making. It involves a systematic review process of identifying and critically evaluating research from various sources and disciplines. Evidence review involves the development of a research question, identification of sources of evidence, articulation of criteria for evaluating the research and evaluation of whether the solution worked based on the evidence.

It informs decision making with recommendations for practice of what worked and what didn’t in a specific context, what remains unknown and uncertain.

In 2017 New South Wales (Australia) adopted a container deposit recycling scheme (CDR) with the objective of reducing waste from drink containers. The decision was informed by systematic review of evidence of 47 examples of CDR projects around the world. Evidence included results from research and practice. This evidence review informed NSW policy makers on the impact of CDR schemes on waste reduction, under which conditions they work best and operational feasibility. See full article

Open Innovation

An open innovation platform called Better Reykjavik is enabling the capital city of Iceland to crowdsource solutions to urban challenges. Many suggestions revolve around the common urban themes of transportation, construction, and environmental issues. Reykjavik residents submit proposals or vote on proposals submitted by others. Importantly, every month, Reykjavik’s Mayor’s Office reviews the best ideas for implementation. The platform has grown in popularity because of its relevance. Now more than half the city’s population has participated in proposing or voting on solutions and 20% of the population regularly uses the site. Since 2011, 20,000 registered users have submitted more than 6,800 proposals, of which more than 600 have been implemented.

Behavioural Insights

Using insights about human behaviour from psychology, cognitive science, and social science, we can design and test solutions, including new policies and services, that encourage individuals to make better decisions. Behavioural insights involve understanding behaviours related to an issue, prioritising key behaviours to change in order to achieve an outcome, and empirically testing the effectiveness of behaviour change strategies using using a randomized controlled trial.

Behavioral insights aim at improving the welfare of citizens and consumers through policies and services that are formed based on empirically-tested results. This ability to test what works is key to implementing solutions effectively. It has been used across public services to generate low cost interventions to improve outcomes. Behaviour change can also make a big contribution to solving complex social, environmental and organisational problems.

In 2013 the Environment Protection Authority Victoria (EPA) was facing a decline in the payment of litter fines even though the fines for litter thrown out of cars were increasing. The EPA tested behaviour change approaches such as personalisation and loss aversion on the information that was presented to the offenders in order to encourage on-time payment. In addition, it modified the process for submitting statutory declarations to reduce the number of people avoiding payment. The trial showed an average increase of 13% on the people who were avoiding late fines by paying on time, and an 18% reduction in the number of people submitting statutory declarations.

Lean Agile Methods

Previously stalled, Denmark switched to re-developing its online business registration system using an agile approach. That is to say, they developed the project in modular bursts with frequent testing of prototypes on real users, and within three years were able to reduce the average time needed to resolve a customer’s problems over the phone from 16 minutes to 5 minutes and dropped the number of customers needing phone support from 70 percent of applications in 2009 to only 30 percent today.

Impact Evaluation Methods

The Sacred Heart Mission in Victoria conducted in 2009 a randomised controlled trial to test the impact of the program "Journey to Social Inclusion" on chronic homelessness and social inclusion. The program consisted of providing intensive support to long-term homeless. Beneficiaries of the program were randomly selected and compared to a ‘control group’ (similar group that did not receive the program). After two years, results showed that the program had a positive impact on housing retention, physical well-being and savings for the government of up to $32,080 per participant. The program had no impact on drug use and jobs. Learnings from the pilot were used to refine and expand the second phase of the project which is currently being tested.

Systems Thinking

The Australian National Outlook project, led by CSIRO and the National Australia Bank, brings together leaders from more than 20 organisations across business, research and civil society to produce integrated economic, social and environmental modelling that paints the big picture for a national conversation about Australia's future to 2060. The project combines CSIRO's research methodology in integrated modelling with social, environmental and economic data and expert perspectives, to consider where Australia might be in decades to come. This systems modelling is used as the basis for proposing five major system shifts - in industry, energy, urban, land and culture.
